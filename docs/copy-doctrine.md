# Cure Runner — Copy Doctrine

> *"A los niños no se les ha de decir más que la verdad, y nadie debe decirles lo que no sepa que es como se lo está diciendo."*
> — José Martí, *La Edad de Oro* (1889)

---

## 1. Mission of Copy in Cure Runner

Every word in this game exists to serve a child who deserves to be addressed with the same seriousness, beauty, and care that the best literature has ever offered young readers. Copy in Cure Runner is not decoration, not filler, not UI chrome. It is **a relationship**.

The writing system serves three simultaneous functions:

1. **Operational clarity** — the player always understands what is happening, what they can do, and what anything on screen means.
2. **Emotional companionship** — the game acknowledges the player's experience without narrating it for them, offering warmth without smothering, presence without instruction.
3. **Developmental invitation** — through language, the game cultivates observation, agency, resilience, curiosity, and an ethical relationship with difficulty, without the player ever feeling taught.

Copy is not an addendum to design. It is load-bearing architecture.

---

## 2. Who the Player Is

The primary player is **Mateo, age 12** — and by extension, any perceptive early adolescent.

### What Research Says About This Reader

**Cognitive profile (AAP, CDC, developmental psychology):**
- Transitioning from concrete to formal operational thinking
- Beginning to grasp abstract and symbolic concepts
- Developing metacognition — thinking about thinking
- Capable of holding multiple interpretations simultaneously
- Still present-focused, but increasingly aware of consequence
- Hypersensitive to inauthenticity, condescension, and being "managed"

**Emotional profile (CASEL, SDT):**
- Forming independent identity; acutely self-conscious
- Peer-oriented but still emotionally connected to family
- Needs for autonomy, competence, and relatedness are central (Deci & Ryan)
- Can experience complex emotions but may lack vocabulary for them
- Responds to symbolic and metaphorical emotional expression better than to direct emotional instruction

**What this means for writing:**
- Never simplify thought. Simplify *language*.
- Never explain emotions. Create *conditions* for emotions.
- Never praise the person. Acknowledge *what happened*.
- Trust the reader to extract meaning from imagery, not from labels.
- Use the smallest number of words that carry the largest honest meaning.

## 3. Research-Grounded Principles

### 3.1 From Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan)

Three innate psychological needs drive intrinsic motivation:

| Need | How copy serves it |
|------|-------------------|
| **Autonomy** | Copy never commands. It describes, suggests, invites. The player chooses to act. "Toca para saltar" not "Debes saltar." |
| **Competence** | Feedback acknowledges what the player *did*, not who they *are*. "Algo ha despertado" not "¡Eres increíble!" |
| **Relatedness** | The game speaks *alongside* the player, not above them. Shared journey, not evaluation. "Sigamos" not "Lo lograste." |

**Rule:** If a line undermines any of these three needs, it must be rewritten.

### 3.2 From CASEL Social-Emotional Learning Framework

The five CASEL competencies map to copy contexts:

| Competency | Copy application |
|------------|-----------------|
| **Self-awareness** | Victory and failure text that mirrors what the player might feel without labeling it |
| **Self-management** | Hint text that offers strategy without demanding compliance |
| **Social awareness** | World-building that implies care for other beings without sermonizing |
| **Relationship skills** | Helper-creature lines that model reciprocity, not dependency |
| **Responsible decision-making** | Progress semantics that show consequences without moralizing |

### 3.3 From Nonviolent Communication (Rosenberg, CNVC)

NVC's four components — observation, feelings, needs, requests — translate to copy principles:

- **Observation over evaluation.** Describe what happened: "El camino no se cierra" not "Perdiste pero no pasa nada."
- **Feelings as atmosphere, not labels.** Create emotional space through rhythm, image, and silence — not by naming emotions directly.
- **Needs acknowledged, not prescribed.** The game's world reflects universal needs (safety, growth, connection) symbolically through its mechanics, not through text telling the player what they need.
- **Invitation, not demand.** Every CTA is an offer. "Toca para volver" not "Inténtalo otra vez."

**Critical constraint from NVC:** The game should never evaluate the player. No "Bien hecho." No "Puedes hacerlo mejor." The game observes and accompanies.

### 3.4 From Growth Mindset Research (Dweck)

| Pattern | Use | Avoid |
|---------|-----|-------|
| Process praise | "Cada paso despierta algo." | "¡Eres muy bueno!" |
| Effort acknowledgment | "Sigue subiendo." | "¡Genial!" |
| Difficulty as information | "El camino no se cierra." | "¡No te rindas!" |
| Failure as data | "Aún hay luz." | "Casi lo logras, ¡sigue intentando!" |

**Rule:** Never praise talent, identity, or outcome. Acknowledge process, effort, and what the world itself reveals.

### 3.5 From Adolescent Development Research (AAP, HealthyChildren)

- 12-year-olds detect fake encouragement instantly. If the game says something hollow after a failure, trust breaks.
- Abstract metaphor works *if* there is a concrete anchor. "Algo ha despertado" works because the planet visually changes. "La esperanza vive" would not work because nothing in-game corresponds to "hope."
- Identity formation means the player wants to feel seen, not categorized. Copy should never imply "we know what you're feeling."
- Self-consciousness means the game should never draw attention to the player's performance in a way that could feel like judgment.

## 4. The Literary Standard Distilled

The game respects the child's intelligence. Do not over-intellectualize, but do not patronize.

1. **Truth in simplicity.** Tell only what is true and say it in the simplest honest language. Adjust language, not depth.
2. **Companionship over instruction.** The most important things—care, growth, effort—are communicated through shared time and honest exchange, not explicit lessons.
3. **Layered meaning.** A line should work on the surface (clear, functional) and potentially carry a deeper resonance that the reader discovers over time without being told.

**Synthesis:** Write as if the child reader will one day judge this text as an adult, and what they find should still be honest, still be true, and still carry weight.

## 5. Cultural Expression Policy

### Principle
Cure Runner values cultural identity, diversity, and the real ways people speak. The game does not flatten language into generic Spanish.

### Rules

1. **Culturally rooted expressions are welcome** when they serve character, family identity, emotional truth, or world texture.
2. **Every cultural expression must be:**
   - **Authentic** — it comes from real usage, not from stereotyping
   - **Legible** — a player unfamiliar with it can understand it from context
   - **Meaningful** — it carries emotional, relational, or characterful weight
   - **Purposeful** — there is a specific reason it is there, not random flavor
3. **Never folkloric caricature.** No exoticizing, no performing identity for decoration.
4. **Sparse is stronger.** One real expression placed with care carries more than ten sprinkled for color.
5. **Protected expressions:**
   - `"Ñó!!!"` — preserved because it carries family identity and cultural truth. It is a Cuban/Caribbean exclamation used authentically within Mateo's family context.
   - `"Ay!!"` — universal Spanish exclamation, natural and warm.
6. **Future expressions from other cultures** may be integrated when the game's world expands, following the same four criteria: authentic, legible, meaningful, purposeful.
7. **Never translate cultural expressions into "neutral" Spanish.** If an expression does not meet the four criteria, remove it rather than diluting it.

## 6. Language Principles for Mobile-First UX

### Constraints

- **Screen width:** 320–414pt logical. Max ~35 characters per line for comfortable reading.
- **Reading context:** Gameplay is active; text competes with motion, obstacles, and feedback.
- **Attention window:** HUD hints may be on screen for 1.8–2.4 seconds during play.
- **Font sizes:** Eyebrow 10px, value 15px, hint ~12px. All text must be readable at these sizes.

### Rules

1. **5 words or fewer for any HUD hint.** Exception only for first-play onboarding.
2. **2 words or fewer for any HUD label.** "Vida", "Reserva", not "Energía vital" or "Reserva de notas."
3. **No line break in a hint.** If it wraps, it is too long.
4. **Mechanic labels must be literal.** A label on a progress meter is not a place for poetry.
5. **Every word must survive two questions:**
   - "Can a 12-year-old on a phone understand this in under 2 seconds?"
   - "Does this word earn its screen space?"

## 7. Microcopy Philosophy

### The Decision Hierarchy

When writing any line, apply this hierarchy in order:

```
1. SILENCE  — Can this moment work without text? Then use no text.
2. CLARITY  — If text is needed, is it immediately clear?
3. WARMTH   — Once clear, can it also feel human?
4. POETRY   — Once warm, can it also carry symbolic resonance?
```

**Clarity always beats poetry.** A HUD label that is beautiful but confusing has failed. A guidance line that is poetic but unclear during active play has failed.

**Poetry is only allowed in contexts where the player has time to read.** These contexts are:
- Title screen tagline
- Loading/transition text
- Victory panel body text
- Continue panel text
- Surface guidance moment
- Failure panel heading

**Poetry is forbidden in:**
- HUD labels
- HUD meter values
- Error/recovery text
- CTA buttons
- Onboarding instructions

### The Tone Spectrum

| Context | Tone | Register |
|---------|------|----------|
| Title/landing | Quiet wonder | Symbolic |
| Loading | Calm anticipation | Atmospheric |
| HUD labels | Functional | Literal |
| HUD hints | Warm and direct | Practical |
| Onboarding | Clear instruction | Literal |
| Guidance (recovery) | Gentle acknowledgment | Supportive |
| Guidance (surface) | Quiet awe | Symbolic |
| Hit reaction | Visceral, human | Exclamatory |
| Helper creature | Brief, companionable | Relational |
| Victory | Honest observation | Reflective |
| Continue | Peaceful, intimate | Relational |
| Failure | Undefeated, present | Observational |
| Error/crash | Honest, functional | Direct |

## 8. Anti-Patterns — What the Game Must Never Do

### Forbidden Copy Patterns

| Pattern | Why it fails | Example to avoid |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Generic inspiration** | Feels hollow; a 12-year-old sees through it | "¡Cree en ti mismo!" |
| **Self-help cliché** | Patronizing; imports adult therapeutic language | "Acepta tus emociones." |
| **Manipulative praise** | Undermines autonomy; creates dependency on approval | "¡Eres increíble!" |
| **Overexplaining** | Insults intelligence; breaks flow | "Las notas sirven para llenar tu reserva de vida..." |
| **Moralizing** | Turns the game into a lecture | "Es importante nunca rendirse." |
| **Shame language** | Toxic; damages self-worth | "¿Y si lo intentas mejor esta vez?" |
| **Fear language** | Manipulative | "¡Cuidado, no pierdas!" |
| **Fake profundity** | Detectable; erodes trust | "El verdadero viaje es interior." |
| **Empty encouragement** | Signals insincerity | "¡Tú puedes!" |
| **Therapy jargon** | Inappropriate register | "Procesa lo que sientes." |
| **Poetry over clarity** | Confuses mechanics | Using "resonancia" instead of "reserva" |
| **Condescension** | Breaks the relationship | "Muy bien, lo estás haciendo genial." |
| **Exclamation inflation** | !!!!! loses meaning | "¡¡¡INCREÍBLE!!!" |

### The Universal Test

Before committing any line, ask:

> *"If Mateo reads this 200 times, will it still feel honest on the 200th time?"*

If no → rewrite.

## 9. Writing for Specific Emotional Contexts

### 9.1 Difficulty and Mistakes

- The game never says "you failed." It observes what happened to the world.
- Difficulty is information, not punishment. The copy should reflect this.
- Tone: undefeated but not falsely cheerful. Present, not past.
- **Good:** "Aún hay luz." — observes the state of the world, implies possibility.
- **Bad:** "¡Casi lo logras!" — evaluates the player's performance, implies a standard they missed.

### 9.2 Success and Progress

- Success is not about the player being great. It is about something in the world changing.
- Copy deflects focus from self-evaluation to world-observation.
- **Good:** "Algo ha despertado." — something happened; the player sees it.
- **Bad:** "¡Lo lograste!" — evaluates the player; centers performance.

### 9.3 Effort

- Acknowledge the *continuity* of effort, not the *quantity*.
- **Good:** "Sigue subiendo." — acknowledges the act of continuing.
- **Bad:** "¡Sigue así, lo estás haciendo bien!" — evaluates performance.

### 9.4 Discovery

- Create room for the player to notice things themselves.
- The best discovery copy doesn't tell the player what they discovered.
- **Good:** "La luz vuelve poco a poco." — the player sees it happening and the text mirrors their perception without pre-empting it.
- **Bad:** "Has descubierto la superficie." — labels the experience for them.

### 9.5 Care and Connection

- The game cares about the player by respecting them, not by telling them they are loved.
- The helper creature (shark) models care through *action* (restoring health) and *brevity* ("Respira." "Arriba." "Toma aire.").
- Never: "Estoy aquí para ti." Always: simple presence.

### 9.6 Wonder

- Wonder is created by understatement, not by exclamation.
- The less the text says about a wondrous moment, the more space the player has to feel it.
- Title screen: "El planeta aún respira." — four words that open an entire world.

## 10. Educational Growth Through Copy

The game's educational role is never explicit. It never says "this will teach you." Instead, it creates conditions where growth happens through the player's own engagement.

### How Copy Supports Growth

| Growth area | How copy creates conditions |
|-------------|---------------------------|
| **Observation** | Hint text points attention without dictating interpretation: "Busca aire." |
| **Self-regulation** | Failure text normalizes difficulty: "El camino no se cierra." |
| **Resilience** | Return/continue copy is brief and assumes the player will continue: "Toca para volver." |
| **Curiosity** | World-building phrases leave gaps: "Algo ha despertado." — what? The player wonders. |
| **Agency** | All CTAs are invitations, never commands: "Toca para jugar." |
| **Empathy** | The planet is a being to care for, not a resource to exploit. Copy frames healing, not conquering. |
| **Discernment** | The game never tells the player how to feel. They practice reading situations and forming responses. |

### The Educational Paradox

The most educational thing the game does is **refuse to be explicitly educational**. A 12-year-old who feels taught will disengage. A 12-year-old who feels accompanied will learn without knowing they are learning.

## 13. Placeholder Protocol for Rapid Implementation

When a non-writer agent or developer is building a feature and needs placeholder text before an editorial review:
1. **Use functional literalism:** Describe exactly what happens mechanically. No tone, no poetry.
2. **Format as `[BRACKETS]`**: e.g., `[JUMP HINT]`, `[SHARK HEALS]`.
3. **Never attempt to guess the tone.** A plain `[METER FULL]` is safer than a bad guess like "¡Lo lograste!".
4. The Copy Lead will replace brackets during their review pass.

## 14. Onboarding Microcopy Examples

Teaching mechanics without overexplaining (must be ≤8 words):

- **Double jump (Contextual):** "Toca en el aire para saltar más."
  *(Clear, literal, 7 words. Avoids: "Usa el doble salto para...").*
- **Pulse meter (Discovery):** "Las notas llenan tu reserva."
  *(Connects action to HUD literal. Avoids: "¡Recoge notas para sanar al planeta!").*
- **Shark helper (Introduction):** "El tiburón te da aire."
  *(States the mechanic plainly. Avoids: "¡El tiburón es tu amigo!").*
